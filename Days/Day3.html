Day 3: Echoes of Kurukṣetra

Genesis at Kurukṣetra

The journey brings us to the very birthplace of the Viṣṇu Sahasranāmam. At the exact location where Bhīṣma lay on the śarapañjara (bed of arrows), the 149th chapter of the Mahābhārata was revealed.

The Six Prelude Ślokas

  • Obstacle Removal: Meditate on Viṣṇu as śuklāmbaradharaṃ (wearing white), moon-colored (śaśivarṇaṃ), with four arms holding the śaṅkha and cakra.
  • Lineage of Vyāsa: Gratitude is offered to the lineage: Vasiṣṭha → Śakti → Parāśara → Vyāsa → Śuka. Vyāsa is recognized as viṣṇu rūpāya—a manifestation of the Lord Himself.
The Victorious Lord: Mahāviṣṇu is victorious over both external foes (like Kaṃsa) and the internal cycle of saṃsāra.

The Birth of Parīkṣit & Kaliyuga

After the war, Aśvatthāmā targeted the womb of Uttarā with a Brahmāstra. Kṛṣṇa entered her womb to neutralize the weapon, saving the child Viṣṇurāta (later known as Parīkṣit because he "examined" everyone to find the form he saw in the womb).

The Five Abodes of Kali

As Kaliyuga began, King Parīkṣit encountered Kali tormenting the bull of Dharma (standing on only one leg—representing Satya or Dāna). Kali was granted five places:

  1. Dyūtaṃ: Gambling
  2. Pānaṃ: Alcohol
  3. Striyaḥ: Licentiousness
  4. Sūnā: Butchery
  5. Suvarṇa: Gold (excessive attachment)
The Curse: Influenced by the gold in his crown, Parīkṣit insulted Sage Śamīka and was cursed by the son, Śṛṅgī, to die in seven days. This led to the narration of the Śrīmad Bhāgavatam.

The Writing of the Mahābhārata

Vyāsa Bhagavān dictated the epic to Gaṇeśa near Badrināth in the Vyāsa Guhā. A unique contract was formed: Gaṇeśa would not stop writing, but he must understand every verse before penning it.

Silence of the Sarasvatī

The Sarasvatī river flowed so loudly that Gaṇeśa could not hear the dictation. Vyāsa requested her to go underground (antah-salilā) to facilitate the transmission of this sacred knowledge.

Complexity as Strategy: To gain time to compose, Vyāsa would dictate "knotted" (kūṭa) ślokas that forced Gaṇeśa to pause and reflect.

Yudhiṣṭhira’s Six Questions

In the Śānti Parva, a grieving Yudhiṣṭhira approaches the "epitome of dharma," Bhīṣma, with six fundamental inquiries:

  • Kim ekaṃ daivataṃ: Who is the one supreme deity?
  • Kiṃ parāyaṇaṃ: What is the one ultimate refuge?
  • Stuvantaḥ kaṃ: Who should be praised?
  • Kam arcantaḥ: Who should be worshipped?
  • Ko dharmaḥ paramo: What is the supreme dharma?
  • Kiṃ japan mucyate: Chanting whose name brings liberation from saṃsāra?

Bhīṣma’s Wisdom: Food & Dharma

Addressing why he remained silent during Draupadī’s humiliation, Bhīṣma explains that adharmic food from Duryodhana had clouded his mind. Having bled out on the arrows, his mind was now pure and dharmic.

Answering the Call

  • The Lord of Time: He is Jagatprabhu—the Lord of all that is born and dissolves. He is Anidhanam (without end) and Anantam (limitless in time, space, and object).
  • Worship & Praise: One must worship Mahāviṣṇu daily. He does not ask for gold, but a leaf, flower, fruit, or water (patraṃ, puṣpaṃ, phalaṃ, toyaṃ).
Example of Vidura: Kṛṣṇa ate banana skins from Vidura because they were offered with pure love, rejecting the ego-filled feast of Duryodhana.

The Supreme Refuge & The Lotus-Eyed Lord

Bhīṣma concludes that the highest dharma is to praise the Lord with love. Bhagavān is the Mahad Bhūtam—the raw material of the entire universe.

Puṇḍarīkākṣa (The Lotus-Eyed)

His eyes are red like lotuses for two reasons:

  1. Reflecting joy for His devotees or anger toward enemies.
  2. Reflecting the image of Mahālakṣmī, who is reddish in color and resides in lotuses.
The Final Answer: Mahāviṣṇu is the source of all maṅgalaṃ (auspiciousness). Chanting His names allows even animals—like Gajendra or Jaṭāyu—to achieve liberation.
