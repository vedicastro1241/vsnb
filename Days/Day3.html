Day 3: Echoes of Kurukṣetra

The Prelude and Lineage of Vyāsa

The discourse begins with the recognition of Vyāsa Bhagavān, the compiler of the Mahābhārata. His lineage is a divine chain of wisdom:

Lineage: Great-grandson of Vasiṣṭha → Grandson of Śakti → Son of Parāśara → Father of Śuka.

Vyāsa is identified as vyāsāya viṣṇu rūpāya—a manifestation of Viṣṇu Himself. He is the one who divided the single Veda into four and composed the Purāṇas to make Vedic knowledge accessible.

Removal of Obstacles

Before any sacred work, one meditates on śuklāmbaradharaṃ (the one clad in white) with a prasannavadanaṃ (benign smile) to remove all vighnas (obstacles).

The Survival of the Lineage

After the Kurukṣetra war, the Pāṇḍava lineage faced extinction when Aśvatthāmā directed a Brahmāstra toward the womb of Uttarā. In her desperation, she prayed to Kṛṣṇa: pāhi pāhi mahāyogin.

Kṛṣṇa entered the womb to neutralize the weapon, saving the child. This child was named Viṣṇurāta, but became known as Parīkṣit because he "examined" (parīkṣā) every face he met, looking for the divine form he had seen while in the womb.

The Arrival of Kaliyuga

As Kṛṣṇa departed, Kaliyuga began. Parīkṣit encountered Kali tormenting a bull (Dharma) standing on only one leg, representing Satya (Truth) or Dāna (Charity).

The Five Abodes of Kali

  • Dyūtaṃ: Gambling.
  • Pānaṃ: Alcohol consumption.
  • Striyaḥ: Licentiousness.
  • Sūnā: Animal butchery.
  • Suvarṇa: Excessive attachment to gold/wealth.

Kali influenced the King's mind through his golden crown, leading to the incident with Sage Śamīka and the subsequent curse by Śṛṅgī. Parīkṣit was destined to die from the bite of the snake Takṣaka within seven days, which turned his focus toward ultimate liberation.

The Writing of the Mahābhārata

Vyāsa sought Lord Gaṇeśa as his scribe for the great epic. The collaboration involved a unique intellectual contract:

  • Gaṇeśa's Condition: Vyāsa must never stop dictating.
  • Vyāsa's Condition: Gaṇeśa must understand every verse before writing it.

To gain time to compose new verses, Vyāsa would insert complex philosophical ślokas that forced Gaṇeśa to pause and reflect. This took place near Badrināth, where the Sarasvatī river was requested to go underground (antah-salilā) so its noise wouldn't disturb the dictation.

The Six Questions of Yudhiṣṭhira

Within the Anuśāsana Parva, Yudhiṣṭhira asks Bhīṣma six fundamental questions that prompt the Sahasranāmam:

QuestionMeaning
Kim ekaṃ daivataṃ?Who is the one supreme deity?
Kiṃ vāpyekaṃ parāyaṇaṃ?Who is the ultimate refuge?
Stuvantaḥ kaṃ?Who should be praised?
Kam arcantaḥ?Who should be worshipped?
Ko dharmaḥ paramo?Which is the supreme dharma?
Kiṃ japan mucyate?Chanting whose name brings liberation?

Bhīṣma’s Final Teachings

Bhīṣma addressed why he remained silent during Draupadī's humiliation. He explained that his mind had been clouded by the adharmic food provided by Duryodhana. On the bed of arrows, that "bad blood" had drained, leaving him in a pure state to teach.

The Nature of Mahāviṣṇu

He identifies Viṣṇu as Anantam (Limitless) across three dimensions:

  • Time: He is anādi (without beginning) and anidhanam (without end).
  • Space: All-pervading, like fire present everywhere even if only visible in one spot.
  • Objects: Residing in all things, from gold to wood.
The Lesson of Vidura: Bhagavān accepts even banana skins if offered with pure love. Devotion (bhakti) is the only requirement for worship.
