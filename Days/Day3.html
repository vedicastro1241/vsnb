By Shri. Ramabhadran V Guruji

Day 3: Echoes of Kurukṣetra

Journey to the Birthplace

The Sahasranāmam was born at Kurukṣetra, where Bhīṣma lay on the śarapañjara (bed of arrows). This journey reflects the transition from war to the highest spiritual instruction.

Refining the Prelude

We revisit the six preliminary ślokas. They emphasize that Mahāviṣṇu is śuklāmbaradharaṃ and prasannavadanaṃ, smiling benignly even in the midst of the battlefield’s aftermath.

The Lineage: Nārāyaṇa → Brahmā → Vasiṣṭha → Śakti → Parāśara → Vyāsa → Śuka.

The Last Hope of the Pāṇḍavas

After the war, Aśvatthāmā directed a Brahmāstra at the womb of Uttarā. She cried out, "Pāhi pāhi mahāyogin!" Kṛṣṇa entered the womb to neutralize the weapon, saving the child who would be known as **Parīkṣit** (the one who examines), because he searched for that divine form in every person he met.

The Curse of Kaliyuga

Kaliyuga began the moment Kṛṣṇa left. Parīkṣit encountered Kali tormenting a bull (Dharma) standing on one leg—symbolizing that in this age, only Truth (Satya) or Charity (Dāna) remains. Kali was granted five abodes:

  • Dyūtaṃ: Gambling
  • Pānaṃ: Alcohol
  • Striyaḥ: Licentiousness
  • Sūnā: Animal Slaughter
  • Suvarṇa: Gold/Excessive Attachment

Influenced by a golden crown, Parīkṣit insulted Śamīka Maharṣi, leading to the curse that he would die in seven days—a catalyst for the narration of the Śrīmad Bhāgavatam.

The Collaboration at Māṇā

Vyāsa sought a scribe for the Mahābhārata, and Brahmā suggested **Lord Gaṇeśa**. The agreement had two unique conditions:

  • Gaṇeśa: "I will write only if you never stop dictating."
  • Vyāsa: "You must only write once you fully understand the meaning of each verse."

To buy time to compose, Vyāsa inserted complex "knots" (kūṭa-ślokas) that forced Gaṇeśa to pause and reflect. This took place near Badrināth, where the Sarasvatī river was ordered to go underground (antah-salilā) to ensure silence for the dictation.

Yudhiṣṭhira’s Quest

Grief-stricken by the war, Yudhiṣṭhira asks Bhīṣma six fundamental questions in the Śānti Parva:

  1. Kim ekaṃ daivataṃ? — Who is the one supreme deity?
  2. Kiṃ vāpyekaṃ parāyaṇaṃ? — Who is the ultimate refuge?
  3. Stuvantaḥ kaṃ? — Who should be praised?
  4. Kam arcantaḥ? — Who should be worshipped?
  5. Ko dharmaḥ paramo mataḥ? — What is the supreme dharma?
  6. Kiṃ japan mucyate? — Chanting whose name brings liberation?

The Influence of Food on Dharma

Draupadī questioned why Bhīṣma remained silent during her humiliation. Bhīṣma’s profound answer reveals a core spiritual principle: **Food is the mind.**

"I was eating the food of Duryodhana. Because my body was nourished by adharmic source, my intellect was clouded. Now, as I lie on this bed of arrows, that bad blood has drained, and my dharma has returned."

This emphasizes that for a seeker, the source and purity of food are as important as the practice itself.

Mahāviṣṇu: The All-Pervading

Bhīṣma answers that Mahāviṣṇu is the answer to all six questions. He is **Anantam**—unlimited by time, space, or objects.

The Lotus-Eyed Lord

He is called Puṇḍarīkākṣa. His eyes are red like lotuses because they reflect the "forest of lotuses" that is Mahālakṣmī, or because of the intense love He feels for His devotees.

The Ultimate Refuge

Bhagavān is the source of all auspiciousness (maṅgalaṃ). The river Gaṅgā itself is the water that washed His toe in Brahmaloka during the Vāmana avatar. By chanting these 1,000 names, one crosses over all sorrows (duḥkhātigo bhavet).
