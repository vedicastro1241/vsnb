Day 4: Divine Remedies & Path of Viṣṇu Sahasranāmam

Recap of Yudhiṣṭhira’s 6 Questions

Bhīṣma established Lord Nārāyaṇa as the supreme destination by answering the fundamental questions in order (6, 4, 3, 5, 2, 1): 1. Who is the supreme deity? 2. Who is the ultimate refuge? 3. Who should be praised? 4. Who should be worshipped? 5. What is the best dharma? 6. Chanting whose name brings liberation?

Bhagavān as Creator, Sustainer, and Annihilator

Śloka 17 describes the Lord as the source of all beings. All jīvātmās—humans, birds, animals, and cattle—emerge from Him at creation and return to Him during pralaya (dissolution).

The Nature of Pralaya: Annihilation is not destruction but a return to original form. Like a mud pot returning to mud when broken, jīvātmās return to an ātmic stage where they do not manifest in physical bodies.

This follows the Taittirīya Upaniṣad, where Bhṛgu asks Varuṇa to define Brahman: that from which everything comes, by which everything lives, and into which everything returns.

Removal of the Fear of Sins

Bhīṣma invites Yudhiṣṭhira (bhūpate) to listen to the 1000 names to remove the fear of pāpa. All people worry about sins committed across millions of births. Yudhiṣṭhira specifically feared the sin of killing his gurus and cousins.

Kṛṣṇa’s Saṅkalpa and Sauśīlyam

The Sahasranāmam was the saṅkalpa (divine intention) of Śrī Kṛṣṇa. He knew Kaliyuga was coming—an age of vice—and intended this as the easiest way to achieve welfare.

Bhīṣma did not "create" these names; they are qualities (guṇas) given by sages across three yugas. One primary quality is Sauśīlyam (Easy Approachability). Examples include:

  • Guha: A hunter befriended by Rāma.
  • Sugrīva: A monkey.
  • Vibhīṣaṇa: A rākṣasa.
  • Gajendra: An elephant.
  • Jaṭāyu: A bird.

Technical Architecture of the Mantra

Spiritual progress involves: Listening, Reflection (Manana), and Action. Manana is like a cow chewing its cud; deep reflection makes one a Mahāmuni.

Nyāsam: Body as a Temple

Nyāsam means "giving up" or "installing." We install the Divine into the head, mouth, and spiritual heart (right side of the chest, shaped like a downward-drooping lotus). This purifies and "charges" the body-vessel.

Mythology of the Right Ear

Touching the lips is unhygienic, so a purification ritual is required before touching the chest. The right ear is a universal purifier containing the Gaṅgā:

The Descent: Viṣṇu's toe (Trivikrama) reached Satyaloka → Brahmā washed it with kamaṇḍalu water (Gaṅgā) → Lord Śiva caught her in matted locks → Bhagīratha penance → Sage Jahnu consumed her after she washed away his hermitage → Released her through his right ear. Thus she is called Jāhnavī.

Bījam: Installed on the navel (nābhi), representing our source and life from the womb. Purpose: To lead us beyond the saṃsāra cakra.

Tools for Karma Yoga

Bhagavān "mixes" the inert physical body with the sentient jīvātmā. The body is not for enjoyment (mental level) but for Karma Yoga (offering service). Once the Divine is invoked, the practitioner concludes by touching their feet in worship.

Kīlakam: The Three Equipments

  • Śaṅkha (Conch): The mouth/speech for chanting names.
  • Nandaka/Cakri: The body/sword for pūjā and service.
  • Cakra (Discus): The mind, rotating and focused on the Divine.

Upanishadic Chariot Analogy

Horses: 5 Sense organs (pañcendriyas) running toward sense objects.
Reins: The Mind.
Charioteer: The Intellect (Kṛṣṇa).
The Goal: The intellect must hold the mind (reins). Example: A diabetic tempted by sweets (mind) must listen to the intellect (knowledge of health) to reach the destination.

Sincerity and Meditation

Meditation should proceed from the known (the universe) to the unknown. Chanting is for the pleasure of Mahāviṣṇu (Mahāviṣṇu-prītyarthe). When the Lord is pleased, He acts as a happy father, rewarding the devotee without being asked.

The Farmer and the Oil Lamp

Nārada once felt superior for chanting 24/7. To teach him, the Lord pointed to a farmer who chanted only 25 minutes a day. He then asked Nārada to carry a full oil lamp around the world without spilling a drop. Nārada succeeded but forgot to chant once because he was focused. The Lord explained the farmer was superior for remembering God despite his heavy worldly burdens.

Key Q&A Facts:
  • Ladies: May perform Nyāsam (except 3-day monthly period).
  • Rules: No strict niyamas. Chant in car, office, or kitchen. Morning, noon, or night.
  • Bhūta: Means both the 5 elements and the Jīvātmās.
  • Obstacles: Even Rāma and the Pāṇḍavas faced them; the lesson is to fight through them.
